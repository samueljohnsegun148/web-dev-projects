Early Life History

Unlike many people, my parents could not afford to enroll me in a public school, so I attended a typical village school, where I was taught in a local dialect (Yoruba) from Primary one to Primary 6. Well, some people would say that they were not born with a silver spoon. Like I would always say

“ they are better off for at least having a spoon. I was neither born with any. Not even with a wooden spoon, talkless of a bronze or silver. ”

Any way, I thank God for at least attending a school. My grandfather, who never went to school for a day, taught me how to read and write. Yes!!! I could read and write, but to compose a simple sentence in English Language was a hard task, aside from the general “come”, “go”, “see”, etc. My teachers were good, but whether they can speak English or not is what I can't tell.

I always watched my peers who went to private schools in amazement when they communicated in English. I remember asking myself when would I become one of these people, too. They were all like a tiny “white god” to me who should be worshipped because they could speak English fluently while I was not. Although I topped the class throughout my elementary education after I had repeated Primary One, I needed to put in extra effort to be able to communicate in English. So, don’t be surprised if you ask me what my greatest achievement is if I told you it is the ability to Speak English. Read more...

Education

I studied Electrical and Electronic Engineering from the University of Ilorin and graduated with Second Class Upper. I realized that I found it easy to teach others and they usually get to understand whatever I taught them.

So, after my graduation, during my National Youth Service Corps (NYSC)- a mandatory national youth service in my country, I decided to enroll for a course in Arts and Social Science. This time around, it was a postgraduate diploma in education, where I took courses such as Philosophy of Education, Educational Psychology, History of Education, Sociology of Education, etc.

I found the programme very interesting, first because it is totally outside my technical background and also because it helped me to learn the process involved in human learning. During this time, I was also teaching in a private Secondary School and also as a teaching assistant in a Federal Polytechnic. It was therefore of utmost interest to me applying what I was learning from the programme and seeing it working in the classes that I took.

After this programe, I took an offer to work as a Health and Safety Officer in one of the largest power companies in my country. In the course of relating with workers, I began to notice a myriad of social problems and suddenly I realized that I needed to go back to engineering school, After all, the essence of engineering is to solve human problems.

Specifically, one of the issues that came to mind then was the challenges usually confronting the independence and the autonomy of the people with blindness and low-vision. I enrolled immediately for a master’s degree in Electrical and Electronic Engineering in the University of Ibadan. I devoted of my school hours to researching on technologies to alleviate the conditions of people with visual impairments.

My current PhD research is still along this direction in one of the top universities in North America. Now, I am learning web development with SkillHat and one of my goals is to learn how to develop accessible and interactive user interfaces for the blind. To be continued …
